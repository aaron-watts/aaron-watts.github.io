Fairphone - Smartphone

dth Month, Year

Fairphone, based in Netherlands, brings something back to smartphones that most people don't even realise is missing. Their approach promotes user ownership of a device, and longevity.

Smartphones are amazing. The Nokia 3210 was released the year I started senior school. If you had told me back then that in less than a decade, people would be walking around with computers in their pockets I would not have beleived it. But I feel as though there is a lot wrong with smartphones today. In this article I will talk about some of the issues I have with todays smartphones and how the Fairphone tackles some of those issues.

Longevity

One big issue I have not just with smartphones, but with most tech nowadays, is that it's all made to be so disposable. Very often, a device can be operational, but it's manufacturer stops giving it updates, or the relatively simple apps that it uses insists on adding more and more bloat until the device can't help but stagger. This results in a lot of otherwise good devices making their way to landfill.

Now, I have to be careful with what I say here, as I don't want to oversell or misinform. Fairphones are designed to last. However, the Fairphone 4 has just been discontinued, after being released in 2021. They were unable to support Android 14 on the hardware, and it looks like the product has been shelved a little early as a result. However, they say they will continue to sell spare parts until 2028, and will provide software until 2026, with a hope of extending to 2028. This means the phone will have had an entire shelf life of either 5 or 7 years. That's 1 year less, or one more, than a typical Samsung depending on which it ends up being. With this in mind, it is clear they are trying to hold true to their goals of longevity, and hopefully they are gaining knowledge with each release. The Fairphone 5 is set to receive security updates until 2031, which, unless it also encounters an issue along the way, should see it supported for 8 years. The Fairphone 4 was the companies first entirely modular phone, so really this should be considered the first of this generation of fairphones.

Repair

Another huge irk I have with smartphones, is how costly it is to repair. They need to be repaired in shop, and it's not cheap. Fairphones are modular and easily repaired at home. Ifixit rate the Fairphone 4 and 5 as 10/10 for repairability.

Ownership

You can make phones more affordable by entering into a contract deal, which normally gives you better value for money on data, minutes and repairs. But you are also now locked in to a monthly payment. These also come with strict limitations on what you are allowed to do with your phone, void the contract and lose the perks.

Fairphone provide instructions on unlocking the bootloaders of their phones, so you can put your own operating system on it. They want you to be able to do this! They are also the device of choice for Murena, a smartphone and cloud provider that promotes itself as privacy friendly while removing as much of Google from the Android operating system as it reasonably can.

By default Fairphones ship with stock Android. Android as an operating system offers the user far more control than iOS, however, the big mobile companies such as Samsung ship their own locked down versions of the Android operating system that take away some of the control of the end user. On a Fairphone, if you don't like an uninstallable app, just disable it. You'd be surprised at what is included in Android that is actually not essential. This has the welcome side effect of massively reducing your data usage, so pay-and-go deals suddenly look far more appealing again.

TIP: another way to reduce data usage in Android is to change your default keyboard - GBoard connects to the internet for it's auto suggest and complete features. You can also disable it's data access in the app section in Android settings.
